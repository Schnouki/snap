Snap eschews the browser’s page change cycle

The most important thing to understand is this: Snap technically makes your website a single-page application; the browser doesn’t change pages anymore, snap does. This implies that:

  • You can’t rely on DOMContentLoaded or jQuery.ready() to trigger your code (use Snap’s events instead).
  • Third-party scripts that rely on them might need tweaking (see Events and script re-evaluation).
  • The browser doesn’t show its loading indicator on page changes, Snap shows its own instead.

The second most important thing to understand is that snap only changes the <body> and <title>, so that your scripts are evaluated only once (this brings a nice, noticeable speed boost in and of itself).

Here is what all of this implies:

  • Your styles and scripts in the <head> should be the same across the pages where snap is on.
  • If something in your <head> depends on the page’s content (like scripts running on page load or CSS animations), it’ll need a little bit of tweaking.

Snap is progressive enhancement: If a visitor’s browser doesn’t support snap your site’s links will work as usual, just without a speed boost.
